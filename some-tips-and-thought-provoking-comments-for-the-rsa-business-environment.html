    SOME TIPS AND THOUGHT-PROVOKING COMMENTS FOR THE RSA BUSINESS ENVIRONMENT.

    Apr 19, 2020

    PEOPLE HAVE CHANGED THEIR BEHAVIOUR AND ROUTINES AND THESE CHANGES ARE HERE TO STAY.

    IT IS STRIKING TO NOTE THAT ON THE LAST DAY OF THE ORIGINAL LOCKDOWN THAT PEOPLE HAS FLOCKED TO TOWN TO DO SHOPPING. EVEN HANDYMEN WITH LDV LOADS OF WORKERS AND MATERIAL WERE ON THE ROADS. 

    BELOW FOLLOWS SOME HINTS AND FACTS THAT CAN HELP BUSINESSES TO ADJUST THEIR STRATEGY AND BUSINESS PLANS. 

    The Core business no longer a holy cow.
    Many businesses have experienced implosions of their production and/or supply of services, or even the deferring of deliveries. The only cure for this distress is to adapt. 
    The positive side is that FAO has published its Food Price index which shows that world food prices are declining.
    FIFO!
    This adaption is discussed below. 
    There are long-term incentives to making sanitisers and face masks. Global leading companies are switching away from their core business to manufacture these products. Some companies are in a position to “slip in” these processes into their normal operations. Examples of such companies are Sasol (petrochemical). Also, Fonterra, Danone, Beingmate which are all dairy companies.

    Transport in the UK has dropped by 67% during the lockdown. The same is expected in the RSA. Transport businesses and their clients must look for other alternatives. 
    Car sales in the EU have plunged by 55,1% during lockdown. I am sure that the car manufacturers did not see this one coming. 
    There also the alcohol beverage companies that are switching their resources to manufacture non-alcoholic beverages. Non-alcoholic sales are up 58% y-o-y in the Western world. “1 in 5 prefer non-alcoholic drinks.”  There will be a major shift to “grown up” simple soft drinks. 
    The e-commerce sale of alcohol and fresh foods has surged in China during the COVID-19 pandemic. These changes are predicted to stay. Hence the impact on retailers and stores will be significant. 
    The digital economy has arrived, forcing businesses, e.g. retailers to shift towards client attracting vs waiting for clients to do business with them. Businesses have not viewed online shopping as important, as only 1,4% of South Africans were buying online. That is before the Virus. 
    The lockdown has exposed the fragility of people’s access to essential goods and services. It is also likely that the number of people losing income, will grow. Food shopping procedures must be restructured (a nudge from the state?). More attention should be given to the urban informal settlements with a high population density.
    As people are staying at home due to the COVID-19 pandemic, restaurants & fast foods are closed, baking at home has become a favourite task. Baking now at home does not only make food for the family, but also relieve boredom. Expect that quarantine at home will boost demand for powdered products. The demand for powdered products has increased by 80% during the COVID-19 outbreak. 

    Sales of instant and long-life foods like:
    - Frozen meals.
    - Instant noodles.
    - Processed meats.
    - Seafood.
    - Milk.
    have surged during the lockdown, especially via e-commerce.  
    Businesses have now realised that they will have to be more client-attracting, e.g. retailers now offer to deliver same day orders.
    Businesses should plan for a rental war, involving landlords, and their clients. On a larger scale the JSE listed retail giants have offered landlords 20% of their rent, until it is business as usual for everyone involved. The Landlords Association (RLA) made a counter offer of between 15% and 100% temporary discount (interest free) on rentals, including small businesses e.g. restaurants, etc. A few of the examples of the retail giants are:
    - Pepkor.
    - Truworths.
    - Mr. Price.
    - Woolworths.
    Finally: businesses must keep in mind that if they decide to do business online (and have all the technical support) it does not mean that their clients will be ready for doing business online:
    - The clients struggle to balance family time with work time.
    - The clients do not have the physical environment to do business (some do not have a study, work in the kitchen, in bed,etc.
    - The clients do not have the necessary infrastructure to participate, e.g. they have no computer, etc.

    Racism in South Africa

    15 Sep, 2020 •
    The following quote in The New York times says it all: “In the history of civil rights, South Africa lays claim to a momentous achievement — the demolition of apartheid and the construction of a democracy. But for black South Africans, who account for 75% of this nation of roughly 55 million people, political liberation has yet to translate into broad material gains.” (P.S. Goodman, The New York Times, 24/10/2017) It is then no wonder that M. Mduli of News24, 14/09/2020 states that B-BBEE has not delivered meaningful empowerment, even in business. Instead of fixing the damage, racial inequality has been on the rise, making South Africa the most unequal country in the world. In the top ten South African (JSE) companies 68% of directors of the top companies still are still males. White people are still dominant at 72%. (Sources, websites of the top 40 companies on JSE & M. Nyirenda, University of Pretoria, Nov. 2010, “Board composition in companies listed on Johannesburg Securities exchange.”) If the black people are still worse of than before, then why must other race groups suffer because of B-BBEE and preferential treatment for "previously disadvantaged people" because it clearly doesn't work. The futile attempt by the authorities only causes racial tension between groups. Despite the promises made to the South Africans population, unemployment numbers have grown from 1994 to 2014 by 71%. The unemployment rate is at a record level of 30,1% in 2020 (before Covid-19). The number of unemployed black people has increased during the 20-year period of democracy by 3,2m or by 79%. Almost 60% of unemployed people in South Africa are black after 20 years since 1994. 16 Years after the end of apartheid (2010) black people are still part of the lowest income group in South Africa. A black person earned R10,000-00 per year, whilst a white person still earned 8 times more at R79,500-00 per year. The ratio between black and white pay is 1: 7,95. The situation has worsened by 2015 at B: W = 1: 8,42. After 21 years of democracy, the black population is still the lowest paid race group in South Africa. Why is this so after 21 years since democracy? The number of people living in slums in South Africa has increased by 1,8m over the past 10 years. 18 million people are dependent on social grants. This is 32% of the total population. This number has increased from 5,8 million in 2003 to 18 million in 2020. The number of people in jobs in 2020 is 16,38 million. This means that there are fewer people earning in South Africa than those depending on grants. There still are numerous statistics showing that the trillions of ZAR spent to uplift the black South Africans, had very little effect on a macro/national basis. Where has the money been spent? THE ORIGIN OF RACISM. It is claimed by some researchers that racism is by instinct, however, it has already been scientifically been proven that this is incorrect. Racism is something that has developed over time, and is not present from birth. In South Africa it is possible that that frustration could be leading to aggression against a minority group. This will happen if the state does not remove inequalities fast enough. The question is: has this aggression already started in South Africa? The lack of corrective action and hence poverty for a race group could lead to the privileged group seeing itself as superior to the disadvantaged group. This could lead to aggression by the disadvantaged group. A study has revealed in 1985 that the relationship between black and white people in South Africa is poor due to exactly this. Has this changed after 26 years of democracy? The effect of previous conflict between groups can last for generations. The historical conflicts between Boer (white) and black people has an impact on relations between black and white, which has spread into society. This has also led to distrust between the community and the police. A deterioration of socio-economic conditions of a group will lead to a perception of a lower status of that group. As previously discussed, the socio-economical position of black people in South Africa is still dire, and hence it is possible that the rest of the country could view them as lower status citizens. Then there are the factors that increases group norms: - Racial discrimination frequently in the news. - A group perceives itself as above the other group(s). - The role of parents. - Massmedia. - The members of one group is expected of to maintain a negative view of other group(s). - Groups isolate themselves from other groups. SUMMARY: Factors increasing the probability of racism: - The role of parents! - The violent protests by black people are creating a negative perception at other groups and stakeholders. - The current weak economy is also creating negative attitudes between groups, eg. B-BEE is creating expectations and disappointments (see Mduli above). - Authoritarian personalities and leaders. Is racism part of THE STRUGGLE, or is the national drive to make it better for all? Is it acceptable that the state views itself as a struggle movement? - Needs not satisfied will upset groups. It became evident during the Covid-19 pandemic that the needs of South African people are definitely not met. - Continuation of racial negative perception after attempts to eliminate it. Is the South African state honestly attempting to eliminate racial perceptions, or is it using racism to gain votes? - The maintaining of group norms in the different race groups supports racial perceptions. - Differences of religions. - Race biological differences. - Social and economic differences. - Previous wars. - Number of members represented by groups. - Work reservation. IT MUST BE POINTED OUT THAT THE RACIAL PROBLEM CAN BE SOLVED IN SOUTH AFRICA.

    CONSUMER BEHAVIOUR AFTER LOCKDOWN LEVEL 5 AND 4.

    01 Jul, 2020 •
    Panic buying is happening without any significant signs to the general public. Is it happening without people realising that it has become a reality, or that is the new way of buying? Consumer behaviour has changed because of panic buying. The buying pattern is almost the same as just before the 1994 elections. The only difference now is that panic buying occurs now more regularly than in 1994. It is speculated that the current regular panic buying is caused by a low trust level in the unilateral decisions made by the government. Uncertainty is causing consumers to stock up. These uncertainties are driven by unfamiliarity with court decisions, the tobacco debacle, conflicting news re infection and death numbers, people defying regulations as regulations now is seen to be targeting a small part of the population (who is staying at home now?). The current taxi debacle is not doing any good. The Minister of Transport who is blatantly ignoring the powerful taxi industry by not attending/delaying meetings with the taxi leaders. Retaliation by taxis was to fill up taxis to at least 100%. Even border control is suspect. The question is, do we still have any lockdown that will prevent the spreading of the Covid-19? The harsh treatment of the public by the police and SANDF and subsequent outcomes of investigations also do not create an atmosphere of trust. The rumours about Eskom and diesel shortages make consumers and the public uncertain of what the “real” status is in South Africa. The fact that samples for the Corona virus has a big backlog for testing, samples are thrown away (e.g. thousands of samples have been found thrown away next to a road in the Eastern Cape) also confuses the public and that have led to erratic consumer and public behaviour. Above uncertainties worries consumers and lead to sporadic panic buying. Suppliers like the dairy industry are actually experiencing an increase in sales of long-life products. This increase is currently leading to shortages in supply to the end user. BUSINESSES MUST BECOME “LONG-LIFE” SUPPLIERS. FOCUS ON SUSTAINABILITY.

    EXAMPLES OF CREATIVENESS AS WELL AS PRO-ACTIVITY OF POST-LOCKDOWN/POST-VIRUS.

    29 Apr, 2020 •
    1. RETAILER SALES. Retailers have reported a sharp drop in sales. They must re-invent their way of doing business. Here are a few examples of adaptation for survival: - Launch your own on line delivery boxes as “self-isolation packs”. - Increase focus on shelf stable products, i.e. pantry product type sales. - Deliveries direct to customers/consumers via e-commerce platforms, home delivery. - What about quarantine centres? - Cater for increasing “at-home channels” at the expense of “away-from-home”. Attempt to grow in this context via e-commerce. - Adapt to the behavior change to growing own food, e.g. vegetables, fruit, yogurt, etc. - Utilise the vast amount of data regarding individuals to sharpen up on the customer/consumer in-home, preferably via e-commerce. - Be careful with new product launches. It could be different to what was planned. - Develop sustainable food systems. - Use R&D companies to develop new products on real time. Look for real-time, high resolution, live streaming of product trials (Tetra Pak). - Be aware that many suppliers have moved out of the supply chain as the lockdown has forced the suppliers to close. - What about paid residential deliveries by drone? - Take note of the Nielsen report stating that “pantries are padded with produce as consumers prepare for the long wait caused by Covid-19. 2. SOME OTHER ADAPTATIONS FOR SURVIVAL. - There are hundreds (if not thousands) of businesses that want to move on line. - Could your business survive by adapting a day-by-day, week-by-week, month-by- month way of doing business? - What about engaging with health-focused audiences? - Build an image of cleanliness to a level that creates confidence and a feeling of safeness. - Clean Hotels, guesthouses, etc. must accept that the majority of meals will probably only be done via room service. - Take note that there is a shift to indulgence among consumers. - Try to land agreements with government to supply health related goods. - Realise that people will be hesitant to go to the supplier/service provider and will prefer to do business on line or via e-commerce.     WHAT WILL AN “AFTER THE VIRUS” ECONOMY LOOK LIKE?

    13 Apr, 2020 •
    1. PREAMBLE. Experts are of the opinion that the COVID-19 infections will stop, once the Virus runs out of people to infect! The RSA economy has come to a halt due to travel bans, lockdowns, low oil prices, global economic slowdown, and the recession prior to the “Virus”. The RSA economy is in distress. What now? The BER (06/04/20, Report 13) describes the RSA economy as having a “triple sudden stop”, which are: - A sharp decline in global demand for our exports. - The lockdown leading to a sharp drop in domestic production and demand. - A combination of the two. P. Cairns (09/04/20, “Three scenarios for a recovery) is concerned that the RSA is able to handle the crisis: - Oil price war. - The weak ZAR. Mike Schussler (KYKNET VERSLAG, 09/04/20) estimate the probable job losses due to the Virus lockdown and the extension of the lockdown to be approximately 3 million. I CANNOT UNDERSTAND HOW ONE OF MY CHINESE CLIENTS ARE OFFERING BOXES OF COVID-19 TEST KITS, 114/BOX AT USD 92/BOX BUT WE HAVE NOT ENOUGH KITS TO TEST? SARS has missed their collection revenue by R66 Billion. Add to this that we were in a recession before the Virus, and then the situation will look dire. Also adding the senseless decision to ban alcohol and cigarette sales will do us economic harm, and will really cost the government image somewhat. We do not need all of this, keeping in mind the S&P evaluation of the government credit rating in May 2020 (next month). 2. AFTER. South Africa will enter the post-Virus era with a heavy burden which already exists: - The PMI’s have plummeted to record lows. - Eskom sales are down by at least 9,500 MW. - New vehicle sales are the lowest in more than a decade. - The ZAR has lost +30% against the USD and, important to note, 22% against the Chinese Yuan. - Downgrades by Moody’s and Fitches (remember S&P in May 20). - Land grabbing has flared up. - Looting of stores has increased. - 55 Schools have been burnt or vandalised. - There are daily traffic jams in cities (contempt for state, or unorganised?). - Will the economy be able to recover? ON THE OPTIMISTIC/POSITIVE SIDE: - The curve of COVID-19 infections has already levelled out. - Informal traders have been permitted trade again. - The Chinese PMI has improved significantly which will start the agonising global return to business. - The ZAR will eventually strengthen, albeit slower than after the economic collapses of the past. The credit ratings will dampen strengthening of the ZAR. - There is a common sense of purpose developing in communities, e.g. gangs in the Cape Flats have decided to stop turf wars and to join hands in distributing food parcels. 3. UNFAIRNESS. First of all, there must be reference to B. Curson (09/04/20, “Big businesses left out in the cold”, Moneyweb web site) stating that there is NO TAX relief for large businesses. This despite the fact that these businesses are important in creating and providing employment, paying Vat, PAYE, excise duties, provisional taxes, and many, many more to support the economy. On top of all this, there is low (or no) demand for non-essential goods or services which will create cash flow problems. “SARS appears to have a ‘Hard line’ to large employers during the COVID-19 pandemic. Also, not that additional to the mentioned hardship large factories and deep mines will take a long time to start up and run at normal levels. To add salt to the wounds, it must be noted that the leader of the FF+, Pieter Groenewald (KykNet News, 09/04/20), has objected to the state financially support only BEE businesses, thus the state ignoring the non-BEE businesses, regardless of SMME status. It seems also that the FF+ contributions to the Solidarity Fund will not happen out of protest. 4. PLEASE LET US GO BACK TO NORMAL. The probability that the RSA will go back to normal is low, if not = 0. Above problems, questions and concerns almost guarantee a new business era in the RSA. Business must have their plan B to G ready and in place. C. Pedersen & T. Ritter (HBR, 10/04/20) list a few concerns that business should take serious after lockdown: - A collapse of customer demand. - Emergency regulatory measures by the state remains in place. - Supply chain interruptions. - Unemployment extremely high. - The economic recession gets worse. - Increased uncertainty by all, including business, environment, people. 5. RECOVERY. The monkey is on the back of the private sector. Government does not have the resources to improve the economic environment. S. Khumalo (Fin24, 10/04/2020, “Open fast food retailers say business chamber as Ramaphosa extends lockdown.”) reports on the lockdown and its extension. The argument by the Business Chamber is that fast foods already operate under strict hygiene and safety standards, and that they hence qualify to do business. He also advocates a staggered approach to kick start the RSA economy. The result here is that food companies should also trade, which is great. The optimistic spin-off is that all businesses that are accredited according to specific QA systems may then also trade. Above is also in line with what Wall Street is expecting and planning: - The economy to be re-opened in phases. - This re-opening will start in smaller cities, and towns, that were not severely hit by the Virus. Some of the questions that needs to be asked by business: - Can you regain lost ground? - Re-visit your business plans and strategy. - Can you culturally deal with the crisis and the aftermath? - Look again at projects, renewing, re-inventing, capex to keep up with changes.

    HOW TO DO BUSINESS AFTER THE VIRUS

    29 Mar, 2020 •
    75 years ago the sliding rule was the top calculator, even scientific wise. It was even faster for the first 20 years than the first calculators. The emergence of the digital age has made sliding rulers immediately obsolete. The manufacturers did not even see it coming. Our seven scenarios indicate that that is going to happen 'after the Virus'. Be aware, people only change if the change is traumatic, and that change is happening now. Businesses will slowly go out of doing business, even the ones that are comfortably surviving the Virus. This change in doing business will shock people, because 'who would have thought that this would happen'.

    THE VIRUS: LIFE WILL NEVER BE THE SAME.

    24 Mar, 2020 •
    We were and are not ready for the TOTAL effect of the Virus. No one knows what our environment will look like this year. And the next decade? Scenario planning is needed, we have done seven different scenarios. We heard that there are already organisations doing scenario strategy planning, Sunter style. The government will not be able to protect us on its own. Businesses will have to take up the slack, whilst they must exist profitably. Business environments are going to change, that’s why we are already planning for new changes. The role of businesses has changed. Communities will expect even more involvement or sacrifices from business leaders. Will investors accept that? BUSINESSES WIL HAVE TO CHANGE THE WAY THEY DO BUSINESS, FOREVER.
